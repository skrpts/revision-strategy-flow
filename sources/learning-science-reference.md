## Overview

This reference covers the evidence-based learning techniques that underpin effective revision. Each technique is described with its theoretical basis, practical application, and the strength of evidence supporting it.

## 1. Retrieval Practice (The Testing Effect)

**What it is:** Actively recalling information from memory rather than passively re-reading or re-studying it.

**How it works:** The act of retrieval strengthens the memory trace and makes future retrieval easier. This effect is independent of whether the retrieval attempt is successful — even failed retrieval attempts (followed by feedback) improve learning.

**Evidence base:** One of the most robust findings in cognitive psychology. Roediger & Karpicke (2006) demonstrated that students who practised retrieval retained 50% more information after one week compared to students who re-studied the material. The effect has been replicated hundreds of times across age groups, subjects, and settings.

**Practical application:**
- Close your notes and write everything you know about a topic from memory
- Use flashcards with self-testing (not just reading the answer)
- After each study session, test yourself without notes before moving on
- Attempt practice exam questions under timed conditions

**When to use:** Every revision session should include retrieval practice. It is the single most effective study technique available.

**Common mistake:** Using flashcards passively — reading the question and immediately flipping to see the answer. The benefit comes from the struggle to retrieve, not from seeing the answer.

## 2. Spaced Repetition (The Spacing Effect)

**What it is:** Distributing study sessions over time rather than massing them together (cramming).

**How it works:** Spacing forces the brain to reconstruct the memory each time, strengthening it progressively. Cramming creates a feeling of fluency that decays rapidly. Spaced practice feels harder in the moment but produces dramatically better long-term retention.

**Evidence base:** Cepeda et al. (2006) reviewed 254 studies and found that spacing consistently outperformed massing, with optimal intervals depending on the delay until testing. For an exam one month away, optimal spacing is approximately one week between sessions.

**Practical application:**
- Study a topic, then revisit it 2-3 days later, then 5-7 days later, then 2 weeks later
- Use a spaced repetition app (Anki, RemNote) for flashcard scheduling
- Build revision timetables that revisit each topic multiple times, not once
- Accept that spaced practice feels harder — this "desirable difficulty" is the point

**Optimal intervals:**
| Time until exam | First review | Second review | Third review |
|----------------|-------------|--------------|-------------|
| 1 week | Day 1 | Day 3 | Day 5 |
| 2 weeks | Day 1 | Day 4 | Day 10 |
| 1 month | Day 1 | Day 7 | Day 21 |
| 3 months | Day 1 | Day 14 | Day 60 |

**Common mistake:** Revising a topic once and moving on, assuming it is "done." A single session is not sufficient for long-term retention.

## 3. Interleaving

**What it is:** Mixing different topics, problem types, or subjects within a single study session rather than studying one topic in a block.

**How it works:** Interleaving forces the brain to discriminate between concepts and select the appropriate strategy for each problem. Blocked practice (studying one topic at a time) feels easier but produces an illusion of competence — students perform well within the block but poorly when topics are mixed (as they are in exams).

**Evidence base:** Rohrer & Taylor (2007) found that interleaved practice produced 43% better performance on a delayed test compared to blocked practice, despite students performing worse during the practice session itself. The effect is strongest for subjects requiring discrimination between similar concepts.

**Practical application:**
- Alternate between topics within a study session (e.g. 20 minutes on Topic A, 20 minutes on Topic B, 20 minutes on Topic A again)
- Mix question types when practising — do not sort by topic
- Study related but distinct concepts in the same session to practise discrimination
- Resist the temptation to "finish" one topic before moving to the next

**Common mistake:** Avoiding interleaving because it feels harder and slower. The difficulty is the mechanism through which it works.

## 4. Elaborative Interrogation

**What it is:** Asking "why" and "how" questions about material to deepen processing and create meaningful connections.

**How it works:** Simply reading a fact (e.g. "spaced repetition improves retention") does not anchor it in memory. Asking "why does spaced repetition improve retention?" forces the student to generate an explanation, linking the fact to their existing knowledge structure.

**Evidence base:** Dunlosky et al. (2013), in their thorough review of learning techniques, rated elaborative interrogation as having moderate utility — effective across a range of conditions but requiring some prior knowledge to generate meaningful explanations.

**Practical application:**
- For every key fact or concept, ask: "Why is this true?" and generate an explanation
- For every process, ask: "How does this work?" and trace the mechanism
- For every claim, ask: "What evidence supports this?" and evaluate it
- Connect new information to concepts you already understand

**When to use:** Particularly effective for factual and conceptual material in sciences and social sciences. Less applicable to purely procedural knowledge (e.g. mathematical procedures), where worked examples are more effective.

## 5. Self-Explanation

**What it is:** Explaining your reasoning aloud or in writing as you work through problems or study material.

**How it works:** Self-explanation makes thinking visible, exposing misconceptions and gaps that passive reading conceals. Students who self-explain learn more deeply because they are forced to reconcile new information with their existing understanding.

**Evidence base:** Chi et al. (1989) found that students who self-explained while studying solved twice as many problems as those who did not. The effect is robust across ages and subjects.

**Practical application:**
- After reading a paragraph, explain what it means in your own words before moving on
- When solving a problem, narrate each step and why you chose that approach
- When reviewing a flashcard, explain why the answer is correct, not just what it is
- Teach the material to an imaginary student — if you cannot explain it clearly, you do not understand it

## 6. Dual Coding

**What it is:** Combining verbal information with visual representations (diagrams, charts, concept maps).

**How it works:** Information encoded in both verbal and visual formats creates two independent memory traces, making retrieval more likely. The act of creating a visual representation also forces the student to identify relationships and structure in the material.

**Practical application:**
- Draw diagrams and concept maps from memory (not copied from the textbook)
- Create timelines for historical or sequential content
- Use flowcharts for processes and decision trees for classifications
- Sketch graphs and tables to represent quantitative relationships

## Technique Comparison

| Technique | Effectiveness | Ease of Use | Best For | Utility Rating |
|-----------|-------------|-------------|----------|---------------|
| Retrieval Practice | Very High | Medium | All subjects | High |
| Spaced Repetition | Very High | Medium | Long-term retention | High |
| Interleaving | High | Low (feels hard) | Discriminating similar concepts | Moderate-High |
| Elaborative Interrogation | Moderate-High | Medium | Factual and conceptual material | Moderate |
| Self-Explanation | Moderate-High | Medium | Problem-solving, reasoning | Moderate |
| Dual Coding | Moderate | High | Visual and relational content | Moderate |

## What Does NOT Work

For completeness, these popular techniques have been shown to be ineffective or low-utility:

- **Highlighting / underlining:** Creates an illusion of engagement without deep processing
- **Re-reading notes:** Produces familiarity, not understanding; one of the least effective techniques
- **Summarising:** Only effective if done from memory (at which point it becomes retrieval practice)
- **Cramming:** Produces short-term performance gains that decay rapidly; antithetical to spaced repetition
